Mai Jiaying Xu

I am a second-year Master’s student in Psychology at Brandeis University, mentored by Dr. Raymond A. Knight. My thesis examines how patterns of alcohol use predict sexual aggression recidivism across different post-release timeframes, using archival data from the Massachusetts Treatment Center and a National Institute of Justice follow-up study.

In parallel, I am involved in several meta-analytic projects focused on youth mental health. At Harvard University, I work with Dr. John Weisz on school-based intervention outcomes (SMILE Project) and the role of psychotherapy homework in youth treatment effectiveness (Big4 META Project). I also assist Dr. Mark Shuquan Chen at Yale University on a global meta-analysis of 20,000 studies examining emotion regulation and mental health across cultural contexts.

My research interests center on the early detection and intervention of severe mental health disorders, with a focus on the developmental pathways leading to their onset or relapse. I aim to identify early indicators and explore preventative strategies to mitigate adverse outcomes, particularly those emerging later in life.

Outside of research, I enjoy creative activities such as baking, painting, playing the Guzheng, and ice skating. I am committed to fostering warmth and positivity in both my personal and professional life.

Research Experience

Research Assistant, Laboratory for Youth Mental Health (Oct 2024-Present)

Engaging in comprehensive research on youth mental health interventions, with a focus on school-based settings. This includes conducting a meta-analysis of approximately 300 studies addressing social, emotional, and behavioral (SEB) problems in K-12 students, such as depression, anxiety, ADHD, and conduct issues. Responsibilities include testing the overall effectiveness of these interventions, analyzing factors that enhance or diminish their outcomes, and examining the relationship between improved mental health and academic performance. Additional duties involve assisting in a meta-analysis on the impact of homework completion in psychotherapy, with tasks such as data collection, cleaning, management, and preparation of manuscripts for publication. Collaborating with a multidisciplinary team to generate insights into how interventions affect student functioning, academic outcomes, and mental well-being.

Graduate Research Assistant, Prof. Raymond Knight's Lab (June 2023-Present)

During my time in the Knight Lab, I collaborated with Dr. Sonja Krstic on her study examining sadism, psychopathy, and sexual offending, which provided a strong foundation for my independent research. Under the mentorship of Dr. Raymond Knight, I designed and conducted my thesis project, Alcohol as a Predictor of Sexual Aggression Recidivism, using the coded data in the Massachusetts Treatment Center (MTC) Dictionary. The thesis has been submitted for presentation at the ATSA 2025 conference. My work in the lab involved studying the developmental trajectories and typologies of aggressive behaviors, particularly sexual aggression, and psychopathy. I gained experience in data analysis, managing the Multidimensional Inventory of Development, Sex, and Aggression (MIDSA), and contributing to research publications. Additionally, I developed skills in scientific writing, statistical analysis, programming (R, Python, SPSS), IRB protocol development, and CITI training. I also participated in Brown Bag colloquia and contributed suggestions for presentations and manuscripts, including those prepared for ATSA and MIDSA conferences. These experiences have significantly enhanced my understanding of the etiology and outcomes of aggression while honing my ability to conduct and communicate.

Summer Research Assistant, Chinese Academy of Science (May 2021-Sept 2021)

During my time at the Chinese Academy of Sciences, Institute of Psychology, I contributed to research on poverty-related psychological issues, female parenting behavior, and the relationship between income and happiness. As part of Dr. Xiufeng Chen’s project, I was trained in data collection and entry while also providing support in the analysis and writing process for work that was later published in Applied Research in Quality of Life. Beyond my project work, I actively engaged in weekly journal readings and discussions, which deepened my understanding of current psychological research and fostered critical thinking. This experience allowed me to develop valuable research skills, including methodological rigor, data management, collaborative writing, and an appreciation for the broader research landscape in psychology.
